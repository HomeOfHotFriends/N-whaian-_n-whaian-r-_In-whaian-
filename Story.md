

Home: an oral history (first telling)

Before anything rose, there was already something.

People like to say there was nothing, but that’s not right.
There was orange. There was movement. There was play.

They weren’t called anything yet, but we call them blobblings now because we need a word for them. They could move, they could gather, they could drift into each other and away again. They could play, and that was enough for a long time.

But they didn’t have the eye.

Not like we do.

So they didn’t really know where they were. Not in the way that sticks. They didn’t know the difference between themselves and something else in a way that holds. They cared, but it was all kind of… spread out. A blobbling could go off to get food, forget why it left, forget where it was, and then not know how to eat even if the food was right there.

That wasn’t failure. That was just how it was before the eye.

Play didn’t need the eye.
Everything else did.

Then Space Dad was there.

Nobody agrees how. We don’t talk about that properly. It’s not hidden, it’s just… not ours to lay out flat.

What matters is that he was already in the soil of Home, and he wasn’t like the others. He had been something else before. Human, they say. Just before he crossed over, before he became what he is now. The first one who wasn’t grown here to become rhizomic anyway.

And he kept the eye.

Not just sight. Not just seeing. The thread. The “I” that stays.

He wore it strange, too. Backwards. Red hat, tipped the wrong way like he didn’t need to face forward to know what forward was.

The first time “I” was said, it wasn’t him.

That matters.

A blobbling said it.

“I like your hat.”

That’s what we remember. That’s the first one that stuck.

He was already bleeding when he said it. A pig had got to him early, before anyone knew what was what. Took the side of his head clean enough that he shouldn’t have lasted.

He was woozy. Half-gone already. But still there enough to want something, to like something, to say “I” and mean it.

Space Dad didn’t give a speech. He didn’t explain anything. He just took the hat off and put it on him.

That did two things at once.

It gave him the eye.

And it stopped him from leaving.

The hat fused. That’s how we say it. It didn’t sit on his head, it became part of it. Held him together.

That’s Hat Man.

He didn’t change shape after that. That’s why he matters.

Most of us, when we come into ourselves, we shift. We become more like what we were always going to be. The Dicken wasn’t always the Dicken. Others change again and again. Even what counts as a “true self” can move.

Hat Man didn’t.

He stayed an orange blobbling with a hat.

That’s the simplest version of having an “I” that works, and it’s the most stable one we’ve got.

Around then is when things started to… separate properly.

Not all at once. Not like a switch.

The eye spreads, but not evenly. Some take it strong. Some don’t. Some carry it into things that look like thinking, planning, holding onto something across time.

That’s when you can start to do more than play.

That’s when things like study, or practice, or ritual can even exist.

Space Dad had ideas. Some of them were accidents. Some of them weren’t, but he let them look like accidents.

He said something in passing once. Nothing big. Just a thought about growing something properly, on purpose, with others. He didn’t push it. He said it near the right person.

Cinnabun heard it.

Cinnabun is one of those you don’t get twice. Strong eye, but not in a lonely way. Knows how to carry something through other people. You can’t build a shared world without someone like that.

That’s where the first vegetable competition comes from.

We call it a competition because that’s the closest word, but it wasn’t about winning. It was about doing something on purpose, together, and seeing what holds.

That first year did almost everything.

People always underestimate that. They think it was just a good season. It wasn’t.

Most of what you see now, most of what grows, most of what walks that isn’t strictly plantling, came out of that first year. The grass, the way the sky sits, the kinds of shapes things take when they’re left to be themselves properly.

Later years did things too. Variations. Strange offshoots. But that first one set the ground.

Herman was there for all of that, but Herman is the kind who comes into things sideways.

Third year, he goes back to where he first grew something big. Biggest thing he ever made, even if he didn’t mean to. Half of it still buried.

There’s a mat there that wasn’t there before. A welcome mat. That’s funny in itself.

He stands on it. Knocks. Nothing.

Most people would leave.

He doesn’t.

He stays. Just… stays with it. Thirty-two hours and one minute, that’s how it’s told. Enough time that the waiting becomes the thing.

Then it gives.

Not opens. Breaks.

He falls through.

Not into someone’s house. That’s wrong when people say that.

He falls into Rhizome.

Properly.

That’s where the phone is.

He’s excited. Of course he is. He thinks this is connection. He rings everything he can think of. Every place he’s heard of, imagined, guessed.

He gets Earth.

That’s where it turns.

On Earth, they announce Home. They say there’s life here. They say it’s a chance to help, to support, to do something good. Most people believe that. They send what they can.

But the ones deciding where it goes know something else.

They know we came from plants.

They know that matters.

So the help isn’t neutral.

They send animals.

That’s how Safe from Farm begins, whether people know that name or not.

They don’t arrive all at once in one clean story. They arrive in waves of confusion, panic, curiosity, hunger, fear. Some of them are dangerous. Some of them aren’t. Some of them could have just passed through.

Hat Man is first to greet them. Of course he is.

He always is.

That’s when he loses the side of his head. That’s when the hat becomes part of him properly, not just given, but necessary.

After that, everything depends on how you act.

People argue about this part, but we all agree on the shape of it.

If you strike everything, you can survive, but you leave damage behind that doesn’t go away. Hat Man lives, but not fully. He carries that hole forward. You don’t see it as a number, but it’s there. It limits him.

If you learn to tell what needs to be stopped and what can pass, if you don’t make more harm than you have to, something else opens.

There are deeper layers to that time. Harder ones. Tests of skill without the usual help. Places where even Space Dad has to just be good, not clever.

Some say he goes back. Some say he reaches Earth. Some say he faces what runs it. Doesn’t matter how you tell it.

What matters is the ending that holds.

He doesn’t destroy Earth.

He comes back.

He goes under.

He pushes Home just enough.

The rocket misses.

That’s the true version, whether you’ve seen it or not.

After that, it doesn’t all go quiet.

Some animals stay. Some learn. Some fit. Some never do and leave or become something else somewhere else.

Home has to change anyway.

That’s where the breaking work comes in.

Hat Man takes the bulldozer. You’ve seen it.

He’s not smashing things for nothing. He’s making space. Turning structures into something that can hold different bodies than they were built for. Some breaks are careful. Some aren’t. You can tell the difference if you’ve been around long enough.

That’s House of Crash, if you need a name.

While all this is going on, there are the tables.

The singing ones.

Same shapes, mostly. Different rules each time. People treat them like games, but that’s not all they are. They carry things. Old stories, things that might happen, things that never quite did but still feel real.

Space Dad uses them too. Not like us. He listens through them.

And in his home, there’s the machine.

Small thing. You sit in it.

Dream on purpose, just for a bit.

Not to escape. To think differently.

To see what might connect if you let it.

That’s enough for now.

The rest depends on how you handle it.
